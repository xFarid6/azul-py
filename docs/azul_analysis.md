# Azul Analysis & Strategy Guide

## Overview of Play
Azul is a tile-placement game played over multiple rounds.
1. **Drafting Phase (Factory Offers):** Players draft tiles of one color from either a Factory display or the Center. Remaining tiles from a Factory are pushed to the Center. The first player to take from the Center takes the First Player token.
2. **Placement Phase:** Drafted tiles are placed onto one of the 5 Pattern Lines on a player's board. Overflowing tiles fall to the Floor Line, costing negative points.
3. **Wall Tiling & Scoring:** When all tiles are drafted, completed Pattern Lines move 1 tile to the matching color space on the Wall.
4. **End Game:** The game ends at the end of the round where at least one player completes a horizontal line of 5 tiles on their wall.

## Overarching Strategy
- **Prioritize the Center:** Taking from the center can be lucrative but risks negative points if it overflows. Timing is critical to secure the colors you need.
- **Pattern Line Management:** Complete larger pattern lines (4 or 5) efficiently, avoiding partial lines that hold up your colors and limit drafting flexibility in subsequent rounds.
- **Contiguous Scoring:** When placing on the Wall, try to place tiles adjacently. You score 1 point for the tile itself, plus points for contiguous horizontal and vertical lines.
- **Denial Drafting (Hate Drafting):** In a 2-player game, observe the opponent's board. Forcing an opponent to take a huge stack of a color they can't place (or forcing them into the Floor Line) can swing the game heavily.
- **Endgame Bonuses:** Aiming for completing vertical columns (7 pts) and 5-of-a-color (10 pts) is a frequent path to victory.

## Paranoid N-Player Game Evaluation
In games with 3 or 4 players, traditional Minimax needs an adjustment. We use a **Paranoid N-Player** strategy for both the Minimax Bot and Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) evaluations.
The core heuristic function scores a board dynamically by evaluating: `my_score - max(opponents_scores)`. 
This creates a paranoid assumption where the bot evaluates all opponent moves as if they are acting optimally as a unified coalition against it to maximize the score of the leading opponent. This seamlessly scales the AI logic to N-players by consistently defending against the strongest opponent on the board.

## Search Space & Solvability
- **Complexity:** Azul is a perfect information game (once tiles for a round are drawn). However, the number of possible states is enormous due to the variability of the tile bag, the board states (Wall and Pattern Lines), and the varied moves available from factories and the center.
- **Is it Solved?:** Azul is *not* strongly solved from the starting position. The combinations of hidden information (what is left in the bag), combined with the branching factor in drafting, prevents brute-force minimax from start to finish.
- **Endgame Solvability:** Towards the end of the game (when the bag is empty, or only 1-2 rounds are left and the terminal condition of a full row is imminent), the branching factor shrinks. The game becomes completely deterministic if the bag is empty and no more refills are required. In this scenario, retrograde analysis or deep Minimax with Alpha-Beta pruning can "solve" the remaining moves.
